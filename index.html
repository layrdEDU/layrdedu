Study of Cathode Ray Oscilloscope

Aim

To familiarize with the CRO and study its functions — measurement of voltage, frequency and phase shift.

Components & Equipment

CROFunction generatorCapacitor 0.1 µFResistors 3.3 kΩ

Theory

A cathode ray oscilloscope gives a visual representation of electrical signals: the X-axis represents time and the Y-axis the amplitude. The heart of the CRO is the cathode ray tube (CRT) — a vacuum tube generating a narrow electron beam that strikes a fluorescent screen. The signal to be observed drives the vertical deflection plates, while the time-base sweep drives the horizontal plates.

Key front-panel controls: INTENS/FOCUS (beam brightness & sharpness), VOLTS/DIV (vertical scale), TIME/DIV (horizontal scale), AC/DC/GND coupling, trigger LEVEL, and X-Y mode for Lissajous patterns.

Block diagram of a CRO

Procedure

  1. Amplitude: obtain a sharp trace, centre it with the AC–DC switch on GND, feed the signal, count the peak-to-peak divisions and multiply by the VOLTS/DIV setting. Half of V(p-p) gives the peak value.
  2. Frequency: show 2–3 cycles with TIME/DIV, count divisions per cycle, multiply by the time-base setting and divide by the magnification factor — this is the time period T; frequency = 1/T.
  3. Phase difference: set up the RC circuit (two 3.3 kΩ / 0.1 µF sections), feed a 2 V sine, view Vin on CH-A and Vo on CH-B in X-Y mode, and compute the phase from the Lissajous ellipse: sin φ = Y₁/Y₂.

RC Coupled Amplifier

Aim

To design and set up an RC coupled CE amplifier using a bipolar junction transistor and to plot its frequency response.

Components & Equipment

Transistor BC107DC source 12 VCapacitorsResistorsBreadboardSignal generatorCRO

Theory

The RC coupled CE amplifier is widely used at audio frequencies. The base current controls the collector current — a small change in IB gives a large change in IC. For amplifier action the emitter-base junction is forward biased and the collector-base junction reverse biased. R₁–R₂ form a voltage-divider bias that makes the operating point independent of β; the input couples through CC1 and the output is taken from the collector through CC2. RE provides current-series feedback stabilising the Q-point, and CE bypasses signal currents to ground.

Input impedance Zin = R₁ ‖ R₂ ‖ (1+hFE)re and output impedance Zout = RC ‖ RL, where re = 25 mV / IE.

Circuit Diagram

RC coupled CE amplifier — designed component values (Fig 4.1)

Design

Required mid-band gain = 50 → select BC107 (hFE(min) = 100, NPN-Si, audio). DC bias: VCC = 12 V, IC = 2 mA, VCE = 50 % VCC.

V_RC = 0.4·V_CC = 4.8 V → R_C = 4.8 V / 2 mA = 2.4 kΩ → use 2.2 kΩ
V_RE = 0.1·V_CC = 1.2 V → R_E = 600 Ω → use 680 Ω
I_B = I_C/h_FE = 20 µA ; V_R2 = V_BE + V_RE = 1.9 V → R₂ = 1.9/(9·I_B) ≈ 10.6 k → 10 kΩ ; R₁ = 10.1/(10·I_B) ≈ 50 k → 47 kΩ
A_V = −r_c/r_e, r_e = 25 mV/2 mA = 12.5 Ω → R_L = 845 Ω → use 820 Ω
C_C1 ≥ 1/(2π·f_L·R_in/10) = 14 µF → 15 µF ; C_C2 = 6.6 µF → 10 µF ; C_E ≥ 23 µF → 22 µF

Procedure

  1. Test all components; set up the circuit and verify the DC bias conditions (remove input and capacitors while checking).
  2. Connect the capacitors; apply a 100 mV(p-p) sine from the function generator; observe input and output together on the CRO.
  3. Keeping Vin = 100 mV constant, vary frequency from 0 → 1 MHz and record the output amplitude in the table.
  4. Plot gain (dB) vs log f. Mark log fL and log fH at 1/√2 of maximum gain (−3 dB).
  5. Bandwidth BW = fH − fL.

Type your measured V₀ for each frequency — gain, log f, the frequency-response graph and the bandwidth are computed live (pre-filled with the manual's readings).

Result: Designed an RC coupled CE amplifier and plotted its frequency response. Manual values: mid-band gain = 29.5 dB, bandwidth ≈ 1.99 MHz.

FET Amplifier

Aim

To design, set up and plot the frequency response of a common-source JFET amplifier.

Components & Equipment

JFET BFW10DC source 12 VCapacitorsResistorsBreadboardSignal generatorCRO

Theory

The JFET is a unipolar, voltage-controlled device — the gate voltage controls the drain current. Like BJTs, JFET amplifiers come in common-drain, common-source and common-gate configurations; common-source is analogous to common-emitter. Here a self-bias circuit is used: it keeps ID and the transconductance gm relatively constant, giving constant voltage gain. The reverse-biased gate–source junction provides very high input impedance.

Clipping & Clamping Circuits

Aim

To design and set up various clipping and clamping circuits using diodes and observe their characteristics.

Components & Equipment

Diodes 1N4001Resistor 3.3 kΩCapacitor 1 µFSignal generatorDC sourcesBreadboardCRO

Theory

Clippers remove an unwanted portion of a waveform without distorting the remaining part, using the diode as a switch. In a shunt clipper the diode is in parallel with the load. Because of the ~0.6 V diode drop, actual clipping levels shift by 0.6 V (e.g. a +3 V biased clipper clips at +3.6 V). The series resistance is chosen as R = √(Rf·Rr) = √(30k × 300k) ≈ 3 kΩ → 3.3 kΩ.

Clampers add or subtract a DC level without changing the wave shape — a charged capacitor acts as the DC source. Biasing the diode moves the clamping level (biased clamper).

Zener Diode Shunt Regulator

Aim

To set up and study a Zener diode shunt regulator and to plot its line and load regulation characteristics.

Components & Equipment

Zener SZ 5.6Resistor 1 kΩRheostat 1.2 kΩVoltmeter 0–15 VAmmeter 0–100 mADC source 0–30 VBreadboard

Theory

A Zener diode is designed to operate in reverse breakdown: beyond the Zener voltage VZ the current rises sharply while the voltage across the diode stays almost constant — the basis of voltage regulation. The series resistor Rs limits the Zener current; IS = IZ + IL.

Line regulation (fixed IL, variable Vin): regulation begins once Vin ≥ Vi(min) = (RL+Rs)·VZ/RL. Load regulation (fixed Vin, variable IL): the diode stays on while RL ≥ RL(min) = Rs·VZ/(Vin−VZ), i.e. IL ≤ IL(max).

Circuit Diagram

Zener shunt regulator — Rs = 1 kΩ, SZ 5.6, RL = 1.2 kΩ rheostat

Design

Output V_L = 5.6 V → select 5.6 V zener (P₀ = 0.33 W, r_d = 8 Ω @ I_Z = 10 mA) ; R_s = 1 kΩ
% line regulation = (ΔV_L / ΔV_in) × 100 % load regulation = (V_NL − V_FL)/V_FL × 100

Procedure

  1. Line regulation: set IL = 5 mA and keep the load fixed. Vary Vin (7–16 V) and record V₀; plot V₀ vs Vin.
  2. Load regulation: keep Vin = 10 V constant; vary RL to sweep IL 0 → 10 mA and record V₀; plot V₀ vs IL. Mark VNL and VFL.

Zener Regulator with Emitter Follower

Aim

To study the performance of a Zener regulator with emitter-follower output and plot line and load regulation characteristics.

Components & Equipment

Zener SZ 9.12N3055 power transistorR_B 330 Ω / 0.5 WRheostat 820 Ω, 1 AVoltmeter, AmmeterDC source 0–30 V

Theory

Plain Zener regulators waste power, have limited load current, and V₀ changes with Zener current. The zener follower combines a Zener reference with an emitter follower: V₀ = VZ − VBE, so when the input changes, the transistor absorbs the excess voltage (V₀ = Vin − VCE). The Zener now carries only IL/β, and the output impedance is low.

Circuit Diagram

R_L = V₀/I_L = 17 Ω (5 W) or 820 Ω/1 A rheostat ; I_B = I_L/20 = 50 mA ; I_S = I_Z + I_B = 60 mA
R_B(max) = (V_i(max)−V_Z)/60 mA = 635 Ω ; R_B(min) = (V_i(min)−V_Z)/60 mA = 26 Ω → select 330 Ω, 0.5 W

Procedure

  1. Wire the circuit after testing all components.
  2. Line regulation: with IL = 500 mA, vary Vin 0–30 V in steps and record V₀.
  3. Load regulation: keep Vin = 15 V; vary IL 0 → 500 mA with the rheostat and record V₀.
  4. Compute VR = (VNL − VFL)/VNL × 100 %.

RC Phase Shift & Wien Bridge Oscillators

Aim

To design and set up (a) an RC phase-shift oscillator for 1 kHz, and (b) a Wien bridge oscillator with amplitude stabilization.

Components & Equipment

Op-amp 741JFET BFW10 (Wien AGC)DiodePotentiometersR, C components±15 V DCCRO

Theory

Phase-shift oscillator: an inverting op-amp (180° shift) plus three cascaded RC sections contributing another 180° satisfies the Barkhausen criterion (total 360°). Oscillation frequency f₀ = 1/(2π√6·RC); the amplifier gain must be at least 29 (kept slightly higher so oscillations don't die out).

Wien bridge oscillator: a non-inverting amplifier with a series-RC / parallel-RC positive-feedback network; f₀ = 1/(2πRC) and required gain 1 + Rf/R₁ = 3. An n-channel JFET acting as a voltage-controlled resistor with a diode negative-peak detector stabilizes the amplitude: rising output makes the gate more negative, the drain–source resistance rises and the gain falls back.

Phase-Shift Oscillator

Three RC sections (R = 680 Ω, C = 0.1 µF) in the feedback path

Wien Bridge Oscillator

Wien network + JFET amplitude stabilisation

Design

Phase shift: f₀ = 1/(2π√6·RC) = 1 kHz ; C = 0.1 µF → R = 650 Ω (use 680 Ω) ; R₁ = 1.5 k, R_f = 33 k + 10 k pot (gain ≥ 29)
Wien: f₀ = 1/(2πRC) ; C = 0.1 µF → R = 1.6 k (use 1.5 k) ; gain = 1 + R_f/R₁ = 3 ; R₁ = 1 k, R_f = 2.2 k (4.7 k pot) ; R_s = 1 MΩ, C_s = 0.1 µF

Procedure

  1. Verify the op-amp (and JFET) are in good condition; set up the circuit.
  2. Note the frequency and amplitude of the output waveform on the CRO.
  3. Phase-shift: interchange R and C and repeat. Wien: vary the gain pot and confirm the amplitude stays constant.

Waveform Generation using Op-Amp

Aim

To set up and study (a) a triangular wave generator for 1 kHz and (b) a saw-tooth wave generator using op-amps.

Components & Equipment

2 × Op-amp 741±15 V suppliesPotentiometer 20 kΩResistorsCapacitor 0.1 µFCRO

Theory

Two op-amps are used: A₁ as a comparator and A₂ as an integrator. When A₁ sits at +Vsat, the integrator output ramps negative; when the divider point P crosses 0 V the comparator flips to −Vsat and the ramp reverses — generating a triangular wave. Frequency f = R₁/(4·R₂·R₃·C) and peak-to-peak output V₀(p-p) = 2·(R₂/R₁)·Vsat.

A saw-tooth is a triangular wave whose rise and fall times differ. Injecting a variable DC voltage (potentiometer) into the integrator's non-inverting input skews the duty cycle: wiper toward −V lengthens the rise time; toward +V lengthens the fall. Saw-tooth generators are used in time-base and PWM circuits.

Circuit Diagram

Triangular wave generator — comparator (A₁) + integrator (A₂); for saw-tooth, a 20 k pot feeds the A₂ (+) input

Design

f = R₁/(4·R₂·R₃·C) = 1 kHz ; V₀(p-p) = 2·(R₂/R₁)·V_sat = 5 V with V_sat = 13 V
R₁ = 1 kΩ, R₂ = 180 Ω, C = 0.1 µF → R₃ = 13 k (use 12 k) ; saw-tooth: add R₄ = 20 kΩ pot

Procedure

  1. Verify both op-amps; set up the triangular wave generator.
  2. Observe the outputs of both op-amps (square at A₁, triangle at A₂); vary R₁, R₂, R₃ and observe the frequency change.
  3. For the saw-tooth, move the pot wiper both directions and note the rise/fall-time change.

Schmitt Trigger

Aim

To design and set up a Schmitt trigger using an op-amp for LTP = 2 V and UTP = 3 V.

Components & Equipment

Op-amp 741±15 V suppliesResistorsVref source 2.5 VFunction generatorCRO

Theory

The Schmitt trigger is a comparator with positive feedback — it converts any irregular waveform into a square wave ("squaring circuit"). The output switches whenever the input crosses the upper (UTP) or lower (LTP) threshold set by the R₁–R₂ divider and Vref. The threshold across R₂ depends on the output polarity: UTP applies when V₀ = +Vsat, LTP when V₀ = −Vsat.

Circuit Diagram

Schmitt trigger — R₁ = 22 k (design 2.5 k), R₂ = 1 k, V_ref = 2.5 V

Design

UTP = +V_sat·R₂/(R₁+R₂) + V_ref·R₁/(R₁+R₂) = 3 V
LTP = −V_sat·R₂/(R₁+R₂) + V_ref·R₁/(R₁+R₂) = 2 V
With V_sat = 13 V → R₂ = 1 kΩ, R₁ = 22 kΩ (as in the circuit diagram), V_ref = 2.5 V → UTP ≈ 2.96 V, LTP ≈ 1.83 V

Procedure

  1. Verify the op-amp IC is in good condition.
  2. Set up the circuit and switch on the supply.
  3. Feed a sine input and observe input and output waveforms on the CRO; measure UTP, LTP and Vsat.

Astable Multivibrator using IC 555

Aim

Components & Equipment

IC 555DC source 10 VR_a = R_b = 6.8 kΩC = 0.1 µF, C₁ = 0.01 µFBreadboardCRO

Theory

The timing capacitor C charges toward VCC through Ra + Rb. When its voltage reaches ⅔ VCC the upper comparator triggers the internal flip-flop, the discharge transistor turns on, and C discharges through Rb until it reaches ⅓ VCC, when the lower comparator flips it back. The capacitor therefore oscillates between ⅓ and ⅔ VCC forever — no stable state.

t_c = 0.69·(R_a + R_b)·C t_d = 0.69·R_b·C f = 1/(t_c + t_d)

Procedure

  1. Verify the IC and set up the circuit.
  2. Observe the waveforms at pin 3 (output) and pin 6 (capacitor voltage) on the CRO.
  3. Measure tc, td, ⅔VCC and ⅓VCC; compare with theoretical values.

IC 555 Timer in Monostable Operation

Aim

To design a monostable multivibrator using IC 555 and compare its theoretical and practical pulse widths.

Components & Equipment

IC 555R = 10 kΩ, C = 0.1 µFTrigger network (10 k, 0.01 µF, 1N4007)Function generatorCRO + probesBreadboard

Theory

The monostable ("one-shot") has a single stable state: output low, discharge transistor on, C shorted. A negative trigger at pin 2 turns the transistor off and drives the output high; C charges toward VCC through R. When vC reaches ⅔ VCC the upper comparator resets the flip-flop, the output goes low and C discharges rapidly. The high time is:

t_p = 1.1·R·C

Once triggered, the output remains high for tp even if further triggers arrive during the interval; a reset pulse during the timing interval terminates the output pulse.

Circuit Diagram

Procedure

  1. Connect the circuit; switch on the supply.
  2. Apply a 1 kHz, ~9 V(p-p) square wave through the trigger network to pin 2.
  3. View the trigger on CH-1 and the output (pin 3) on CH-2; note the pulse width and amplitude.
  4. Observe the capacitor voltage; compare practical tp with the theoretical value 1.1RC.

Verification of Superposition Theorem

Aim

To verify the Superposition theorem in a DC circuit.

Apparatus

Voltmeter MC 0–30 V × 2Ammeter MC 0–5 AAmmeter MC 0–2 A × 2Ammeter MC 0–1 A × 2Rheostat 19 Ω, 8.5 A × 3

Principle

In an active, linear network with several sources, the response (branch current or voltage) in any branch equals the algebraic sum of the responses of each source acting alone, all other sources being made inoperative — a voltage source is replaced by a short circuit (plus its internal resistance) and a current source by an open circuit.

Limitations: not applicable to circuits with only dependent sources, to non-linear elements (diode, transistor), or to power calculation (power ∝ I² or V²).

Circuit Diagram

Step 1: both E₁ & E₂ acting. Step 2: E₁ alone (E₂ shorted). Step 3: E₂ alone (E₁ shorted).

Procedure

  1. Connect as in figure (A); switch on both DC supplies E₁ and E₂; for different voltage settings note all meter readings (I₁, I₂, I₃).
  2. Switch off; reconnect as (B) with E₁ alone at the same setting; note I₁′, I₂′, I₃′.
  3. Reconnect as (C) with E₂ alone at the same setting; note I₁″, I₂″, I₃″.
  4. Verify I₁ = I₁′+I₁″, I₂ = I₂′+I₂″, I₃ = I₃′+I₃″; then disconnect after switching off.

Interactive Tabular Column

Enter the three sets of ammeter readings (currents from the "alone" steps carry sign — use negative values where the direction reverses). The sums and the verification check update live.

Result: Superposition theorem verified — each branch current with both sources equals the algebraic sum of the currents due to each source acting alone.

Calibration of Thermocouple using Thermometer

Aim

To calibrate the given thermocouple using a thermometer, measure temperature with the thermocouple sensor, and plot the calibration curve (indicated reading vs % error).

Principle

A thermocouple works on the Seebeck effect — joining two dissimilar metals produces a thermoelectric potential at the junction. Electrons diffuse across the interface until the electric field balances the diffusion force; since that force depends on temperature, the junction potential measures temperature:

E₀ = C₁(T₁ − T₂) + C₂(T₁² − T₂²)

Wheatstone's Bridge

Aim

To use Wheatstone's bridge for measuring medium resistances such as DC voltmeters and rheostats of various ranges.

Apparatus

Wheatstone's bridgeVoltmeter MC 0–30 VVoltmeter MC 0–10 VRheostats 750 Ω / 570 Ω / 40 Ω

Principle

The Wheatstone bridge is the standard method for medium resistances (1 Ω – 0.1 MΩ). P and Q are known ratio arms, S a known variable (decade) resistance, X the unknown. At balance no current flows through the galvanometer and:

X = (P/Q) × S, S = S₁×1000 + S₂×100 + S₃×10 + S₄×1

Procedure

  1. Connect the unknown (voltmeter / rheostat) to the external terminals; select internal battery and galvanometer.

  3. For the final balance remove the galvanometer shunt (maximum sensitivity); note the range selector and dial readings.
  4. Repeat with the supply current reversed (cancels thermoelectric emf) and take the mean; repeat for each unknown.

Calibration of Single-Phase Energy Meter

Aim

To calibrate the given single-phase energy meter by direct loading and phantom loading (at upf, 0.5 pf lag & lead, 0.866 pf lag & lead).

Apparatus

Energy meter 240 V, 2.5–10 A, k = 1500 rev/kWhWattmeter 250 V, 5 A upfVoltmeter MI 0–300 VAmmeters 0–10 / 0–5 ARheostats 50 Ω / 9 ΩStop watch1φ autotransformer

Principle

The energy meter is compared with a standard wattmeter + stopwatch. In direct loading the current coils of both meters carry the load current; energy is found from the time for N disc revolutions. Testing at full load wastes energy, so in phantom loading the current coils are fed from a separate low-voltage source (large current, negligible power) while the pressure coils get rated voltage from another source. Different power factors are obtained by taking the pressure-coil supply from a different phase of a 3-φ system: R-phase (upf), Y or B phase (0.5 lag/lead), R–Y or R–B line voltage (0.866 lag/lead).

Indicated energy / rev, IR = (1000×3600)/k watt-seconds True energy TR = W × t(1 rev)
Error = IR − TR % Error = (IR − TR)/IR × 100

Procedure

  1. Apply rated voltage via the autotransformer; adjust load current with the rheostat.
  2. Note voltmeter, ammeter, wattmeter readings and the time for 5 disc revolutions; repeat up to rated current.
  3. For phantom loading adjust the current-coil circuit (~1 A) with minimum resistance; repeat readings.
  4. For 0.5 / 0.866 pf tests connect the pressure coils per the phasor diagrams (lag: Y phase, lead: B phase, etc.).
  5. Compute TR, IR and % error; plot the error curve (I on X-axis, % error on Y-axis).

Measurement of Displacement using LVDT

Aim

To calibrate the given LVDT using a micrometer and plot: (1) actual vs indicated measurement, (2) indicated measurement vs % error, (3) output voltage vs core position.

Apparatus

LVDT (±10 mm) with calibration jigMicrometer

Principle

The Linear Variable Differential Transformer has three coils on a tube — a centre primary and two outer secondaries wired in series opposition — with a movable ferromagnetic core. AC excitation (1–10 kHz) on the primary induces voltages in the secondaries proportional to their mutual inductance with the primary. With the core centred the two induced voltages are equal and opposite → output zero (null). Displacing the core raises one secondary voltage and lowers the other, so the output magnitude is linear with displacement and its phase gives the direction. The frictionless, contactless core makes the LVDT highly reliable — widely used in machine tools, robots and control systems.

LVDT construction — primary, two secondaries in series opposition, movable core

Procedure

  1. Mount the LVDT on the jig with the micrometer at 20.00 mm; align the LVDT core with the micrometer core.
  2. Power on (230 V, 50 Hz) and warm up 10 minutes.
  3. Rotate the micrometer to zero; adjust CAL so the display reads 10.00 mm.
  4. Move to 10 mm and adjust ZERO; move to 20 mm and re-adjust CAL to 10.00 — the instrument is now calibrated for ±10 mm.
  5. Move the core in 1–2 mm steps; tabulate micrometer (actual) vs display (indicated) and the output voltage; plot the graphs.
